Skills, Attributes & Stats

Skills will likely be the biggest decision you'll face when building a character. Much like the Skill trees you may find in certain RPGs, Skills determine the kind of abilities you'll be able to unlock and purchase. These abilities are called Spells for Magic Skills, and Techniques for Physical Skills. In order to learn a Spell or Technique, you must have reached its rank in the appropriate skill, as well as having learned any lower-ranked spell of that Spell line.

Attributes are a resource required to cast Spells and perform Techniques. Attributes are divided into Health (HP), Stamina and Mana. Every character has 20 HP, 10 Stamina and 0 Mana to start with, as well as 20 additional Attribute Points to further increase their maximum Attributes.

Characters with 0 HP are Downed, meaning they can't act on their turn and suffer a wound, reducing their Maximum HP by 5 until they have an opportunity to rest. Characters with 0 Stamina will generally be unable to use Techniques and are at risk of being knocked Unconscious, and characters with 0 Mana will generally be unable to cast Spells, as well as suffering Manashock, which prevents them from Concentrating on effects. Manage your Attributes carefully!

Stats are used to determine various things around combat, such as Accuracy, Damage, Parry, Armor and many more things. Stats are divided into Strength, Dexterity, Vigor, Mind and Willpower, each with their own uses and benefits. By default, each character has a d4 in each of these stats, and 6 Stat Points to increase them with. Using Stat Points to increase a Stat will increase their Die Size by one step, as detailed below. Stats cannot be increased beyond d12+5.

d4 → d6 → d8 → d10 → d12 → d12+1 → … → d12+5
Strength
Determines the damage of your Unarmed and Weapon Attacks, as well as being required to wield certain weapons. When attempting to apply certain Crowd Control Effects to enemies, such as when using a Grab Action, you will typically roll your Strength against the opponent's Vigor.
Dexterity
Determines the accuracy of your Unarmed and Weapon Attacks, as well as being relevant in calculating your Parry. Your Parry is equal to Half of your Dexterity + 2.
Vigor
Determines how durable you are, by granting extra health points, increasing your damage shield cap, giving you more Armor, and determining how resistant you are to physical crowd control. Each level gained increases your Health and Damage Shield cap by 5 points. The formula for Armor gains is Half of Vigor-2.
Mind
Determines the accuracy and damage of Spells you cast. Additionally, your Spell Range increases by 1 for every point of Mind. Spell Range has a Base value of 2.
Willpower
Determines your ability to resist magic and influence your opponent through spells. Your Ward rating equals Half of your Willpower -2, and you roll your Willpower to apply Crowd Control Effects to enemies through spells, as well as to resist them. Your Spell Range also increases by 1 for every point of Willpower.

Roles in Combat

There are five roles in combat that a character can occupy. These roles determine which kind of trial a character must face in order to advance beyond certain threshold levels, as well as signifying to themself and others what they can be expected to do during combat encounters.

⚔ Warrior
Frontline combatants who specialize in protecting others, taking hits in their stead and being tough enough to survive doing so. No warrior can go without Guard effects, such as the Bodyguard or Anchoring Howl techniques, to allow them to redirect attacks aimed at their allies toward themselves.
🗡 Assassin
Masters at quickly defeating dangerous enemies, eliminating threats before they can harm the assassin's allies. High Accuracy and Movement are essential, allowing them to quickly reach enemies and reliably take them down before falling back to avoid retaliation. It's crucial for assassins to understand the threats posed by each enemy and which to prioritize in order to best aid their team.
💥 Destroyer
Powerful combatants that deal damage in large areas, capable of damaging and defeating several units at once. Clearing out weakened enemies while simultaneously damaging untouched enemies and weakening enemies so that others may come in and pick them off are what destroyers are all about.
✶ Support
Backline combatants who typically prioritize healing, buffing and otherwise supporting their allies in favor of attacking enemies. Not only do supports have to be mindful of their allies' HP, but also any Negative Effects that may affect their allies.
⛓ Disabler
Combatants specialized in hindering and even disabling opponents, using a variety of Crowd Control abilities. Disablers might completely remove enemies' ability to move, remove powerful enemies' Poise and otherwise create vulnerabilities for their allies to exploit. Their abilities are indispensable when fighting units with Poise.

What's in a Character Sheet?

Character sheets for TSR consist of two parts; one part Social and the other Combat.

Social Character Sheet

On the Social Character Sheet, you'll be expected to list a number of details about your character's personality, their life and how they appear to others. Though few things are mandatory for the Social Character Sheet, the following are considered must-haves:

  • Name — How do they introduce themself? Do they perhaps use an alias?
  • Race — Is your character human? Or perhaps part of the Vanir race? Or maybe they are a Creature of Darkness, such as a Vampire or a Werebeast?
  • Age — How old do they appear? Are they 41 years old, or do they appear to be in their mid-twenties?
  • Appearance — What do others see when they look at your character?
  • Personality — If someone were to interact with your character, what would their likely first impression be?
  • History — How did they live before becoming who they are today? Anything not approved upon character submission may be vetoed as untrue if it is found to be in conflict with pre-established Lore.

Combat Character Sheet

The Combat Character Sheet is a lot more rigid and factual, by comparison. Anything on the Combat Character Sheet should be as accurate as possible, so that it may be navigated as easily as possible and not cause unnecessary delays in combat.

  • Name — Use this name when making Transaction posts to buy new Spells, Techniques and Equipment.
  • Level — Everyone starts at level 1.
  • Role — Your character's intended purpose during combat.
  • Boon — Your character's chosen starting Boon.
  • Attributes — HP, Stamina and Mana. Everyone has 20 HP, 10 Stamina and 0 Mana to start with, as well as 20 Attribute Points to further increase these as you see fit.
  • Stats — Strength, Dexterity, Vigor, Mind and Willpower. Each starts at a d4, and you have 6 Stat Points to increase these with at level 1.
  • Skills — Everyone has 3 Skill points by default, enough to raise a single Skill to Master Rank, or take 1 Skill at Adept Rank and 1 at Novice Rank.
  • Equipment — Weapons, Armor and other gear purchased from the Items List, and their Carry Capacity. Carry Capacity is equal to the character's Stamina.
  • Money — Everyone gets 500 Silver to spend by default.
  • Techniques — Each character can learn however many Techniques they can afford, but they can only prepare a number of them for combat at any given time. A character has a number of Technique Slots equal to their Stamina. Equipping a Technique requires 2 slots for Novice, 3 for Adept and 4 for Master Techniques.
  • Spells — Each character can learn however many Spells they can afford, but they can only prepare a number of them for combat at any given time. A character has a number of Spell Slots equal to their Mana. Equipping a Spell requires 2 slots for Novice, 3 for Adept and 4 for Master Spells.
  • Reference Section — For ease of use, write down your Parry, Armor, Ward, Movement and your Attacks.

Leveling Up

While not immediately relevant, your character will eventually level up as you survive battles and complete quests. When this happens, you will naturally receive some benefits and grow stronger.

Each character starts with 3 Skill Points, 6 Stat Points and 20 Attribute Points to spend as they wish. Characters require 10 Experience to level up, and upon leveling up, they will receive 1 Skill Point, 1 Stat Point, 5 Maximum HP and 5 Attribute Points. Furthermore, at levels 4, 7 and 10, characters gain an additional 1 Stat Point on top of the regular level up bonuses.

Earning Experience

When a character defeats an enemy, or enemies surrender, the party will earn Experience. Once combat is over, the Experience earned is tallied up and split amongst the party. Once a character receives 10 Experience, they will reach the next level.

Fighting units 1 tier below your own will only grant half Experience, and units 2 or more tiers below your own won't provide any Experience at all.

Tiered Play

Once a character reaches level 3, 6 or 9, before they can earn more experience they must pass a trial based on their role, to make sure they're ready to face the next tier.

Step-by-Step Creation

The easiest way to fill out the Combat Character Sheet starts with your vision; what do you imagine your character would look like in combat? Are they a mighty amazoness with a large axe? A delicate duelist with a slender rapier? Or perhaps an ill-tempered mage wielding powerful flames? Having an idea of what your character would be like can greatly speed up the creation process.

Step 1 — Write down what you already know
Your character's name if you already know it, for example. If nothing comes to mind, you can at least write down that your level is 1.
Step 2 — Choose your Skills and your Role
Based on your vision for the character. There are many skills to choose from, but each of them has been designed with a certain aesthetic in mind, to recreate certain archetypes… Or subvert them.
Step 3 — Determine your Attributes and Stats
Once you know what Skills you want to go for, you'll also have an idea of whether you'd want to play a Stamina-based fighter or a Mana-based caster. Casters typically benefit from high Mana, whereas fighters tend to benefit from Stamina, and Health if they seek out the frontlines. Similarly, Strength and Dexterity are crucial to fighters (and Vigor, if they are tanks), whereas Mind and Willpower should take priority for casters.
Step 4 — Choose Equipment, Spells and Techniques
Once you've figured out your skills, you'll also know what Spells and/or Techniques you'll be able to learn. A blaster mage with high mind will want to learn some offensive spells, and perhaps learn some Spellcrafting to further increase their damage output. A fighter isn't much without their weapon of choice, even if this turns out to be their fists, and a tank wouldn't be very good at taking hits without any armor or a shield.
Step 5 — Pick your Starting Boon and run the numbers
Once you've got all of these things figured out, you should also know your final numbers. You'll be able to determine your Parry based on your Dexterity, your accuracy and damage based on Dexterity and Strength respectively, or your Mind. Your Armor, based on Vigor and your Equipment, and your Ward, based on Willpower and your Equipment. At this point, you can make any revisions if you're still dissatisfied with your character for some reason, or you can submit them for review!

⚡ Combat Primer

A condensed summary of how a combat turn works. New to TSR? Start here before reading the full guide below.

Pre-Battle

Before combat, players roll Dexterity to determine the turn order, and have one last opportunity to ready themselves. During this "pre-battle" phase, players may draw weapons, and use techniques or cast spells to prepare themselves for the fight. Typically this includes self-buffs and defensive abilities, but any action is valid so long as it doesn't move the user or their allies, or affect any enemies.

Your Turn — Upkeep Phase

At the start of your turn, the Upkeep phase happens before anything else. During this phase, you may choose to pay the cost for any ongoing effects on your character. Any effects that you can't — or choose not to pay upkeep for — end immediately.

The next step of the Upkeep phase is the Damage-over-Time phase. In this phase, you may choose to lock in an action to cleanse yourself of any such effects. If you don't, or any of these effects remain afterwards, they trigger immediately, before you can take any other actions.

Your Turn — Actions

During your turn, everything you do is defined as 1 or more actions, and you may take 3 of these actions total. Some actions available to every character by default:

Attack Phases

Whenever you declare an attack, you start an Attack Phase. These come in two types:

  • Player Phase — Attacks on your turn. Can benefit from Offensive Martial Stances (Vicious Strike, Cleave, etc.), but are limited by your available actions and Multiple Attack Penalty (each attack after the first suffers a cumulative −5 accuracy penalty).
  • Enemy Phase — Attacks made as a reaction to what an enemy is doing. Not constrained by action economy or Multiple Attack Penalty, but require an unspent Reaction. Typically Attacks of Opportunity or Counterattacks.

Attack Enhancers

When declaring an attack phase, you may spend attribute points to add up to 2 Attack Enhancers. They last for the entire duration of the attack phase. The default enhancers available to everyone:

Reactions — The Enemy Turn

Once you use a reaction, you cannot use another until it refreshes. Your reaction refreshes when:

  • You deal damage with an Attack of Opportunity or Counterattack
  • You nullify the damage of the first attack that hits you in a phase
  • You successfully avoid an entire attack phase
  • You use the Swift Guard technique (Endurance skill)
  • Your turn starts

Attacks of Opportunity

Certain actions create an opening for a skilled opponent to strike. Units provoke an Attack of Opportunity when: casting a spell, making a ranged attack, moving out of a space within an enemy's reach (willingly), standing up from Prone, or taking an Interact Action. Taking damage from an AoO inflicts Stagger until the end of the unit's turn, and disrupts any spell or item being used.

Player units can only perform Attacks of Opportunity if they have at least Adept in the CQC skill, have learned the Relentless technique (Endurance), or have the Overwatch Marksmanship mastery.

Counterattacks

While in Guard Stance, nullifying the damage of the first attack that hits you in a phase — or avoiding the entire phase — opens the attacker up to a Counterattack. Like Attacks of Opportunity, Counterattacks are regular attacks that cannot be modified by Offensive Martial Stances, but may be enhanced with Attack Enhancers. AoOs and Counterattacks can be part of the same Enemy Phase attack phase, meaning enhancers can carry over between them for free.

Defensives

In addition to attacking, you'll often be forced to defend. Defensive reactions must be declared when you're being targeted by an Attack Phase, and you may only benefit from a single Defensive at a time — it lasts until the start of your next turn. These are unlocked through spells and techniques.

How Combat is Set Up

Combat in TSR is simple to get into, but hard to master. To begin combat, all participants must roll their Dexterity Stat to determine their initiative. Units will take turns based on their initiative, with higher initiative scores acting first. In case of a tie, the unit with the higher Dexterity Stat acts first.

Before the battle begins, all combatants may use a Free Interact Action to draw up to 2 pieces of equipment, and a single Action that can be used for anything so long as it doesn't damage, target, move or otherwise negatively affect any enemy unit. Players with lower initiative may choose to wait with declaring their pre-battle until all units with higher initiative than them have declared theirs. Typical uses for pre-battle Actions include casting self buffs, summon and terrain spells, drinking a potion or entering guard stance.

The Player Turn

Once your initiative comes around, it is your turn! A turn has a few components by default; the Upkeep Phase and 3 Actions. The Upkeep Phase is always the first thing that happens during a turn, after which you can use your Actions to impact the battlefield.

The Upkeep Phase is when units pay Attribute Points to maintain the effects of any self-buffs they have used in prior turns, roll to Break Free from certain Negative Effects, count down the durations of effects with a turn limit, and roll damage for certain Damage-over-Time effects if those are affecting the unit. Notably, Damage-over-Time effects will not inflict damage if a unit uses their first action on their turn to Cleanse them. Certain Spells and Techniques may additionally have effects that activate in the Upkeep Phase, so keep an eye out for those!

Actions are the primary resource that is used for what you can do in your turn. From attacking, to moving, to using an item, each of these motions will use one or more Actions to complete. By default, a unit has 3 Actions on their turn, and they can use these actions in any way they wish.

Free Actions are a type of action that require hardly any time to use at all. They do not use up your Actions, and some of them may even be used during other units' turns. However, many abilities that can be used as a Free Action have some restriction on the situations they can be used in, which will be described in the Spell or Technique itself.

The Enemy Turn

When faced with an incoming enemy horde, what should you do? What CAN you do? There are a number of options, actually. When it is the Enemy Phase you always have a Reaction to be able to react to things the enemies do to you, which you can spend in order to perform a few maneuvers, like Attacks of Opportunity, Counter Attacks, or Defensive Actions. You can also spend an Action during your turn entering Guard Stance to boost your defenses, in order to prepare for the enemy onslaught.

Defensive Actions are a type of Reaction that may be used to shore up your defenses when you're about to be hit by an enemy's attack, which may just save your life. Some Defensive Actions include Dodging, which allows the unit to spend up to 4 Stamina to increase their Parry by half the amount spent, or Blocking, which allows the unit to spend up to 4 Stamina to increase their Armor by half the amount spent. These defensive bonuses last until the user's next Upkeep Phase, though the unit may use additional Defensive Actions to replace their previous Defensive Action. Defensive Actions are obtained through training with Techniques and Spells.

When you successfully dodge all attacks in an Attack phase against you, that counts as a Dodge, or if you're hit but take no Health damage from the first attack, it counts as a Block. If you manage to successfully proc a Dodge or a Block, your Reaction is Refunded.

Attacking

Attacking in TSR refers to any moment where a unit attacks with a weapon or unarmed strike to deal damage to an opponent. To attack an enemy, roll your relevant attack dice + any modifiers (this number will be referred to as your Accuracy), and if the total matches, or is greater than the target's Parry, the attack hits. After it hits, we check to see if it surpasses any resistances the defender may have, if it has, it deals damage.

Once an attack is declared in public, before the attacker rolls their accuracy, the defender has a brief moment where they are allowed to declare a defensive reaction, if they have one, or state they perform no action. After that is declared, the dice is rolled.

Multiple Attack Penalty

Attacking multiple times in a row during your player phase incurs a penalty to the subsequent actions. After the first attack, your second attack will have a -5 Accuracy penalty, and the third will have a -10 penalty. This penalty does not apply to attacks made outside of the player phase, such as Attacks of Opportunity or Counter Attacks. Certain Actions might not directly deal damage, but still qualify as Attack actions, and as such are still subject to MAP, either applying MAP to your next attack when you perform them, or having it applied to them after an attack is performed. Certain Actions also count as several attacks for the purposes of MAP, which will usually be detailed in the action itself.

Critical Hits

A critical hit, or a crit for short, is a lucky hit that manages to strike a target's weak spot. An attack becomes a critical hit when the accuracy roll exceeds the target's Parry by 4 points or more. Spells cannot land critical hits, unless specifically stated otherwise. When you land a critical hit, your attack will deal increased damage — roll one extra weapon die and add it to the damage dealt.

Like attack enhancers, you have a limit of up to 2 spell enhancers that you may use in a single attack phase.

Offensive in nature, these Martial Stances are Actions that can be used to substitute the Attack Action with an alternative effect.

Flanking

Flanking a target is simple. When a unit and at least one other allied unit are within 5ft of the same enemy on opposite sides, that enemy is flanked. Without any bonuses, when a unit is flanking an enemy, they gain a +1 bonus to their Accuracy rolls when attacking that enemy. Casting Magic

Casting in combat is generally more complex than swinging a sword, it takes more Actions to do so, and it leaves you more open as well. Most spells cost 2 Actions at base to cast unless stated otherwise, and leave you open to an Attack of Opportunity if cast near an opponent capable of performing such a feat. To cast magic, you will use your Mind and Willpower, either to attack with concentrated beams of arcane power, or subtly manipulate the minds of your foes with a crowd control effect.

When casting a spell that requires you to hit an enemy with an attack roll, you'll follow very similar rules to swinging a weapon, but instead of using your Dexterity to determine Accuracy and Strength to determine damage, you will use Mind to determine both of those things. When using a spell that puts a negative effect on an opponent which can be resisted by a saving throw you will instead automatically hit with said spell if your target is within your spell's reach and you can see them, at which point they will try to resist your spell by rolling a saving throw. Spells like these are made more powerful with the Willpower stat.

Certain spells however are a mix of both of those things, having a damaging component and a CC or negative effect attached to them. In those cases the spell can be dodged entirely if it does not manage to hit the target's parry. So to be able to deliver their negative effect, they must hit parry, pass resistance and damage shields to do damage, and then their target must fail the save.

Spells are delicate weaves of magic that require concentration and precision to be manipulated, and as such cannot be performed while holding objects on both hands that impede your movements. You must have at least one free hand to cast magic. Some mages are able to learn how to amplify the powers they gain from using their hands, while some others learn how to cast without using their hands at all. Spells such as those that function like attack enhancers, as well as spells that can be cast as a free action, and Touch Magic, can always be cast without needing a free hand.

Novice spells cost 2 mana, Adept spells cost 4 mana and Master spells cost 8 mana. All magical self-buffs may be cast with 1 Action, and take up the caster's Concentration slot. Casters can only have one spell/magical entity they are actively concentrating on at any given time. Casting spells with one free hand gives you a -1 mana cost to all of your spells; this bonus does not stack with two free hands.

Magic Range

Both Mind and Willpower determine the range in which a mage can throw out their spells. At base d4 Mind and Willpower a character has 2 range with any spells they wish to cast, but with every increase in either stat they gain +1 range with their spells.

Touch Magic

Touch Magic is a form of casting created by ancient battlemages, it allows a fighter to pour an offensive spell through a weapon attack to deliver both the physical attack and the spell in question. When using Touch Magic, the caster expends the mana they would have used to cast, and then is able to choose the delivery method for the spell, being able to use their normal spell Accuracy or their Weapon Accuracy if they so wish. Not all spellcasters are capable of using this, only those especially trained can accomplish this feat.

If a spell has a casting cost of 2 or less actions, and it is a single target spell, whether that be damage or crowd control, it can be used in Touch Magic. The attacker will spend 2 Actions to make an Attack with their weapon of choice against their intended target, unloading the spell as a secondary damage source. This counts as 2 attacks for MAP purposes. When casting a spell through Touch Magic and the spell's damage type matches that of the weapon, the delivery method also affects the damage scaling, along with the accuracy. This only applies to the first hit of your attack phase; all other hits use regular scaling.

Summoning

Summoned creatures are powerful tools that many casters use in battle, but they take a toll on the caster's concentration to control and maintain. When a caster successfully summons a creature, they may place it at any free space at a 10ft radius around them, and unless stated otherwise, the creature immediately moves in that turn during their initiative.

When a summoned creature is put on the battlefield, it must be commanded by the caster in order to act. This requires the caster to spend 1 Action every turn, which in turn provides the summoned creature with 2 Actions to use in their turn. Casters can have as many summons as they want, as long as they have the Actions to command them.

Swarm Summons are a type of advanced summoning spells that summon a group of creatures instead of a single one. They are usually weaker individually, but provide the caster with more action economy. Swarms are linked together, taking 1 Action to command, which gives each creature in the Swarm 2 Actions to perform their turn. The difference being that the Swarm creatures can't be given complex commands, or perform specific things, they must all be commanded as a group.

Summons can be controlled from a great range, but it's not unlimited. A rough estimate for when summons begin to lose their vital link to their masters tends to be around 500ft, unless stated otherwise.

Damage & Resistances

Damage calculations are rolled against Physical Resistance, also known as Armor, or Magical Resistance, also known as Ward. Characters can receive these through their Vigor and Willpower stats respectively. For Armor it is Half of Vigor - 2, and for Ward it is Half of Willpower - 2.

There are two forms of acquiring resistances, which constitute Active and Passive resistances. Passive resistances are those that remain active for longer than only one attack, such as physical resistance on a character acquired from armor. Active Resistances are those acquired from an instant effect and are only active during an attack or a defense, such as the physical resistance a character acquires from a block.

Primary and Secondary Damage

A weapon or spell, when they have multiple damage sources to them, will have a Primary Source and a Secondary Source. In the cases of a power attack, or any other attack enhancing effect, they will only apply to the Primary Source. The Primary Source of damage will be the one that acts as a vehicle to deliver the Secondary. In the case of a weapon, the Primary Source is the physical damage of the weapon, and the Secondary Sources are any enchantments that are carried through the physical weapon.

When calculating Secondary Damage, only up to 3 sources of Secondary Damage can be applied to a vehicle of attack. One of these sources must be chosen as the main source to receive full modifiers, while all extra sources will receive halved modifiers. Additionally, while there are other secondary sources of damage such as poisons, the only sources of Secondary Damage that receive the Secondary Damage stat increase are Magical Damage sources such as enchantments, Touch Magic, resins, or other effects that mimic them in nature.

There is however, a separation between Primary Damage and Spell Power when dealing with damage in the game. Primary Damage is a physical stat gained from various martial abilities that will improve your damage with weapons, as well as Strength scaling attacks. Meanwhile Spell Power is a magical stat gained from various spellcrafting abilities that will improve your damage with spells, as well as Mind based attacks. Simply put, Primary Damage will boost the power of weapons, while Spell Power will boost the damage of spells, unless stated otherwise.

Stacked Damage Scaling

When calculating all of these bonuses it's important to note that same source damage scalings on a weapon or spell are always stacked together, and apply as stacked both when considering resistances, and when considering applicable bonuses. Effects that halve damage, such as Glancing or an enemy's Poise, apply to both primary and secondary damage. If the total resulting damage is reduced to a fraction, instead round up to the nearest whole.

Effective Damage

Certain enemy types in the world have particular weaknesses to certain damage types. When a unit is weak to a certain type of damage, weapon attack dice of that type will always be Maxed Out. In the case of a Holy spell targeting a creature of darkness, we max out the spell dice. The basic holy spell Shine, 1d6 + Mind, would turn into 6 + Mind. Now, what if the Effective Weapon manages to crit on top of dealing Effective Damage? The extra dice gained from a critical hit will also be maxed, and if the unit happens to score a Deadly crit, it maxes out their Stat dice as well.

Damage Shields & Armor Penetration

Damage Shields are a secondary pool of Health Points that protect you from injury. They are not like ordinary Health Points, in the sense that reaching 0 Damage Shields does not cause unconsciousness, nor can you receive them if you are unconscious. The damage shields that you can carry into battle are determined by your Vigor. At d4 base Vigor you can have 5 damage shields at any given time, and each time you upgrade your Vigor you add +5 to your damage shield cap.

The damage shields also cannot incur damage from poisons, bleeds, or other DoT effects, unless stated otherwise. If however, a character is affected by a poison or bleed type of effect and puts up a damage shield, they will still take Health damage from that effect, through their damage shield. A damage shield absorbs all damage dealt to a character until it loses all of its health and breaks. A damage shield receives no benefits from passive resistances, unless stated otherwise.

Example: Two targets are hit by ten needles, each dealing 1 point of physical damage. Target one has +1 passive physical resistance, and target two has no resistance, but a +5 damage shield. Target one will take no damage from the needles, and target two will have their shield broken, and take 5 damage from the remaining projectiles.

Armor Penetration

Armor Penetration, or AP for short, is an effect that certain weapons and abilities give you that allow the attacker to penetrate armor, ignoring physical resistance points, and adding the AP value directly to your damage when striking a damage shield. While dealing more AP may seem better than doing more damage at times, AP cannot take your opponent into a negative physical resistance. This means that using AP against heavily armored targets is much more advantageous than using it against no armor targets. AP does not factor in against Physical Resistance gained by blocking, or any spell/effect that replicates a block.

Self Buffs, Augments & Banners

Self Buffs are effects gained by spells and feats that boost your character's effectiveness by giving them temporary stat growths in various areas. There are two types of Self Buffs in the game. Magical Self Buffs are gained from spells, whereas Physical Self Buffs are gained from feats and potions. A character can have at most one Self Buff active at any given time, either Magical or Physical. This is usually mentioned as the "Concentration Slot", and is often shared with one's ability to use Summons as well. Self Buffs may be canceled as a free action during your Upkeep Phase, including before the upkeep cost is paid.

Augments are minor beneficial effects that, unlike magical self buffs, do not take up your Concentration Slot. There are physical and magical augments, but unlike the self buff counterparts, they do not have a dedicated slot, and you are able to stack as many Augments as you see fit.

As a third component of the buffs category, we have group buffs, which we call Banner Buffs, or simply Banners. These will usually be either an Augment that a character will carry, or an object or structure. Both of those cases will emit an aura which provides a buff to allies around it. They are very useful when playing in a group, as they can boost the stats of every person in the squad, not just one. Characters can only benefit from only 2 Banner Buffs at any given time, and they are able to choose which ones they will benefit from at the start of their turn as a Free Action.

Some abilities, when used, have a cooldown attached to them. The cooldown counter is reset at the start of the user's turn, before they take any action. The round that you used the ability with a cooldown does not count towards the cooldown.

Spell & Technique Slots

In addition to carrying weapons, alchemical items and other equipment, there is a limit to the number of spells and techniques a combatant can have prepared at any given time.

Each combatant can prepare a number of Techniques based on their maximum Stamina, and a number of Spells based on their maximum Mana. Spells and Techniques follow the same rules for slot cost, requiring 2 Slots to prepare for Novice, 3 Slots for Adept and 4 Slots for Master. Therefore, a mage with 20 Mana could prepare up to 5 Master Spells, up to 10 Novice Spells, or any other combination of Spells with a total Slot cost no higher than their maximum Mana.

You can further increase the amount of Slots you can use by learning a Training Spell or Technique. You can only learn a single Skill's training, which will grant 5 additional Spell Slots for each of the Training's Spells that you've learned, or 5 additional Technique Slots for each of the Training's Techniques that you've learned, to a maximum of 15 additional Slots when you learn the Master-level training. The Trainings themselves require no Slots to prepare, as they represent your mastery over that specific Skill.

During extended adventures or back-to-back encounters, combatants might want to change which Spells and Techniques they have prepared. In order to do so, they must take at least a Short Rest, to prepare the appropriate materials and re-familiarize themselves with the new spells and techniques. If they are interrupted during this time, they'll be unable to complete the changes and have to start over if they still want to change their prepared Spells and Techniques.

Resisting Crowd Control & Saving Throws

There are two main categories in which all Crowd Control effects, or CC for short, are divided; Physical CC and Magical CC. Physical CC targets the body, including effects like poisons and forced movement. By default, Physical CC is resisted using the Vigor stat. Magical CC targets the mind and spirit, including effects like curses and hallucinations. By default, Magical CC is resisted using the Willpower stat.

In order to affect someone with a Crowd Control effect, you don't roll accuracy like you would during a normal attack. Instead, you can attempt to impose the effect onto the target directly, forcing the target to make a Save. A Save is an opposed roll between the two characters, where the aggressor rolls their stat (typically Strength or Willpower) against the defender's Vigor for Physical CC or Willpower for Magical CC. If both results end up equal, the defender wins, successfully resisting the CC.

Once a CC is successfully applied, affected characters get an additional opportunity to shake off the CC at the start of their turn, during the Cleansing Phase. This is called Breaking Free. To successfully Break Free, they roll their Vigor again for Physical CC, or their Willpower for Magical CC, trying to hit a target number equal to 2 + Half of the Relevant Stat that was used to apply the CC. Until they successfully Break Free or the CC ends on its own, each new attempt receives a cumulative +2 bonus which stacks every turn, increasing their odds of success with each turn they remain on the CC.

Downstate & Resting

When you go to 0 HP or below, you enter Downstate, where your character is in a critical moment where their consciousness is slipping. If that character is healed back up to 1 HP or more, they are back in the fight and able to act upon their initiative order. If they take enough damage to bring their HP into the negatives equal or beyond their current total HP, they will be Unconscious, effectively out of combat.

During Downstate a character cannot perform any actions, and is effectively at the mercy of their allies to help them. Any Self Buffs, Augments, or other similar sustained effects are immediately dropped as soon as a character enters Downstate. As soon as a character hits 0 HP and goes into Downstate, they immediately receive a Wound, lowering their max HP by -5.

Long Rest (~6 hours)
At any point where a character is able to rest and recover for a period of around 6 hours, that constitutes a Long Rest. During a long rest you and your party fully recover any injuries, status effects (unless stated otherwise) and attributes. After a Long Rest the party also recovers from any Wounds they have sustained through their day of battle.
Short Rest
When characters do not have the full 6 hours to rest and recover, they can only perform a Short Rest. During this time they only recover from specifically stated injuries and status effects, and only recover 50% of max attributes. During a Short Rest, each member of the party can only recover from 1 Wound.

Special Weapon Traits & Meeting Requirements

Wielding different weapons, weapon combinations, or even fighting without them isn't simply a choice to make when deciding what will give you the most damage, but it can also affect your character by giving them special benefits in combat.

There's 3 broad categories for physical weapons which all weapons generally fall into. Simple Weapons are very easy to use and can be manipulated by anyone who meets the strength requirements to swing them around, or has the Dexterity to aim with them. CQC Weapons are more complex martial melee weapons made for war, which require specialized proficiency to manipulate properly. Marksmanship Weapons are deadly ranged weapons designed for battlefield use, which also require specialized proficiency to manipulate properly.

Many weapons (Armor as well) will usually display a requirement in their description, usually Strength, to be wielded properly. If you cannot meet the requirements of a melee weapon you are trying to attack with, you lose access to any special properties it may have, and your weapon's damage dice only goes as high as your own Strength. If you cannot meet the requirements with a ranged weapon, you automatically miss all of your shots.

One handed weapons can provide you with the most options, as it allows you to carry a shield, another weapon, or have your offhand open for various tasks. Versatile weapons are heavier than one handed weapons, a bit harder to wield, but can be powerful tools in the hands of those who are able to make use of them. They can be used in one hand, but grant special bonuses when wielded with two hands. Two handed weapons hit harder in single strikes, and are often great at attacking multiple opponents at once. Polearm weapons are a subset of Two Handed weapons; they have an extended striking reach, which means that they can strike from further away, and don't necessarily have to be in melee range to hit their opponents.

Armor follows similar rules when it comes to meeting requirements, as heavier armor will have Strength requirements that you will have to meet, or else they will weigh you down and make it very difficult to perform in combat. Using armor above your requirements will stop you from being able to cast spells, halve your movement speed, give you a 2x penalty to resource cost on all of your actions, as well as a Disadvantage on every action.

Armor is generally divided into 2 weight classes. Light Armor has robes, clothes, or thick leathers that could provide some simple protection, but never burden the user too much. Heavy Armor, for the fighters who will be the frontline of fighting, holding back various enemies at once. The requirements for Heavy Armor are fairly high, and the armor itself tends to be the most expensive, but it provides the best defense of them all.

Ranged Combat

Ranged Combat works differently than melee, and there are a few important points to understand before picking up a bow, crossbow, rifle or even a throwing weapon. When using a ranged weapon to attack, you must keep in mind what type of weapon it is.

Crossbows tend to be the easiest to use as most of them don't require proficiency to fire, and have decently long range. But they have bolt clips that limit how many bolts can be fired each round. Firearms can deal with armor very effectively, as they are the hardest hitters and have armor piercing properties, but have ammunition clips that limit the amount of bullets that can be fired each round. Bows tend to be the most versatile ranged weapons, but the more powerful bows will usually have high stat requirements to be used. Throwing weapons tend to be used as a complement to most melee styles, as they're not as powerful and focused on ranged combat as the others, but can be used quickly in one hand to deal damage to a foe at mid range.

Firing a bow, crossbow, or gun in melee range will provoke an Attack of Opportunity, but this can be avoided with certain skills or weapon combinations. Thrown Weapons can be used in melee combat with no Attack of Opportunity opening, if they are used to hit in melee instead of being thrown in range.

Mounted Combat

As soon as your character saddles a mount, be it a horse, pegasus or wyvern, you are flagged for mounted combat. Riders and their Mounts have separate stat sheets, with Mounts having their own Parry, Resistance, and Health values that need to be tracked and remembered. Mounts also have Actions as well, but they use their Actions alongside their riders in the same initiative order.

Unless stated otherwise, a mount will only have 2 Actions, and they are often limited in what they are able to do with those. In a vast majority of cases, those actions will simply be used to Move, and the rider will have to use one of their own actions to command the mount to do so. The rider can however use more of their Actions to command the mount further, in which case, the mount will gain 1 more Action for each extra Action the rider uses to command it. Riders have access to their Armor, Weapon and Defensive feats normally, except Movement abilities.

Fliers suffer a weakness to physical ranged damage and wind damage while in flight. Mounted units cannot interact with the Grappling System, meaning they cannot be grappled, or grapple other units. Ranged weapons are often awkward to use while flying, so they receive a -3 penalty to the range of their ranged attacks that are fired while Hovering or Flying. Their range is determined exclusively by the amount granted by your skill level or the weapon type, and cannot receive any increases from master perks or attack enhancers.

Flying mounts are able to land or take flight once per turn, during their turn as a free action. They can do it at any point during their turn, but only within their turn, and only once, with the one exception being that they cannot do it while they are in the middle of their movement.

Flight & Battlefield Verticality

Certain units can acquire the ability to fly through certain methods, via their skills or otherwise. In which cases, they will receive a flight status, and their ability will detail a "Flight Speed", which will differ from their base ground speed. The ground speed of flying units, unless stated otherwise is always the default movement speed, and any movement boosts that a unit receives while they have a Flight speed stat active are applied either to their ground speed or their flight speed, not both, the user gets to choose which one.

While flying, a unit does not suffer any negative or positive effects from terrain, unable to be slowed down by forests, deterred by low walls, or damaged by things like burning woods, but they also do not receive Defensive Stat bonuses from things like fortified terrain, pillars, or anything of the like.

Any unit that has access to a flight speed can also Hover, which essentially boils down to ending their turn in mid air instead of on the ground. That can be dangerous however, as taking Primary Effective Damage while Hovering will cause the unit to plummet and fall to the ground, falling 50 spaces (500ft) until they are able to make a Hard Dexterity check to attempt to recover during the start of their turn. Upon a failed check however, the unit continues falling 50 spaces each turn until they're able to recover. The fall will also happen if they are successfully afflicted by any Root, Prone, Stagger, Bind, or Paralysis crowd control effects. While Hovering it's also important to note that because of the often awkward movements of flight, Hovering units receive a -3 penalty to the effective range of all of their ranged attacks.

Falling is a legitimate hazard on the battlefield. A fall from one space, or 5ft, is nothing to worry about, but two spaces and above the damage taken increases. For each space fallen above 10ft, count 1d8 + X (X being the number of spaces above 10) of damage. Gravity Damage is counted as Physical Damage.

Stealth

Stealth is the art of moving silently, without being detected. It is a skill that works differently in a combat situation vs a non combat situation, as in a battlefield all units are on high alert and will not give any potential infiltrators many chances to disappear. While in a non combat situation a stealthy rogue might be able to sneak inside a mansion full of guards completely undetected due to them not paying as much attention.

To make use of Stealth on the battlefield, you'll need a skill that enables you to do so. The most common ones being Smokescreen from Martial Arts, or Invisibility from Figment. Upon meeting the conditions, they are allowed to position themselves outside of the starting deployment area designated for their party. There's two limitations to this: the stealthed unit cannot deploy in an area that is inside of the Perception Radius of an enemy unit, and the stealthed unit can only deploy within what their Movement would allow if they were to use their 3 Actions to Move from within the starting area.

Perception Radius: Every unit has a default Perception Radius of 5ft, which scales with their Willpower. Each point in Willpower makes the radius 5ft larger. In a non combat scenario the Perception Radius is lower, only half of Willpower is added to increase the Perception Radius.

Detection Radius: When using Stealth in an out of combat situation, a few rules apply. You no longer need to use the aforementioned abilities to enter Stealth, and the rules about Perception Radius apply to consider the detection abilities of any patrolling guards. The Default Detection Radius is 5ft, but some things can add to it: Wearing Light Armor adds 5ft, Heavy Armor stops you from being able to enter Stealth altogether. Every 10 units of carry weight add 5ft. Using more than 1 Action to Move per turn adds 5ft per extra Action.

If your Detection Radius overlaps with an enemy's Perception Radius at any point, you have to roll a Dexterity vs Willpower check to remain hidden. If their Willpower matches or exceeds your Dexterity, you are revealed. If multiple Perception Radius circles overlap onto a Detection Radius, each Perception roll is made at +2 for every overlapping circle. Every round a stealthed unit spends overlapping their Detection Radius with a Perception Radius, that unit gains a +2 stacking bonus for their Perception roll.

Stealthed units may also use an Action to Interact with an adjacent terrain feature like a closet, bush, bed, or cabinet. The stealthed unit may hide inside of it, removing their Reveal Radius altogether. At which point, they can only be revealed if another unit Interacts with their hiding place.

Size

Oftentimes you will meet creatures who are smaller or larger than your characters while in combat. A smaller creature will usually be more frail, but harder to hit. While a bigger creature tends to be easier to hit, but also a lot more tough. A normal sized, Medium creature is referred to as Size 1. Every Size above 1 gives +1 to Armor and Primary damage, but -1 to Parry on the creature's base stat block. Creatures who are Size 2 and above also get +5ft of reach on their Melee Attacks.

A creature who is 2 sizes smaller than another cannot score critical hits, forcibly move, knock prone, or initiate grapple checks against the bigger creature, unless stated otherwise. When a creature who is 2 sizes bigger than another moves into a smaller creature, it forces the smaller creature to make Strength vs Vigor save, if they fail, they are forcibly moved away, if they succeed, they stop the bigger unit's movement.

When being Guarded by a smaller creature, a bigger creature needs to be wholly within their Guard aura to be able to be Guarded, otherwise they cannot benefit from a smaller creature's Guard. Bigger creatures have Advantage of grapple checks against smaller creatures.

Squeezing

At times, units may want to move into or through spaces that they normally wouldn't fit in. To do so, they may attempt to squeeze as part of any move action, allowing them to move through any terrain that would fit units at most 1 size smaller than them, but at half speed. While squeezing in this way, units have Disadvantage on attack rolls, and attack rolls against them have Advantage.

Summoning Rules & Limitations

Summoned creatures are powerful tools that many casters use in battle, but they take a toll on the caster's concentration to control and maintain. When a caster successfully summons a creature, they may place it at any free space at a 10ft radius around them, and unless stated otherwise, the creature immediately moves in that turn, during their initiative.

When a summoned creature is put on the battlefield, it must be commanded by the caster in order to act. This requires the caster to spend 1 Action every turn, which in turn provides the summoned creature with 2 Actions to use in their turn. Casters can have as many summons as they want, as long as they have the Actions to command them. Most summons follow this pattern, the most common exception to this rule being Swarm Summons.

Swarm Summons are a type of advanced summoning spells that summon a group of creatures instead of a single one. They are usually weaker individually, but provide the caster with more action economy. Swarms are linked together, taking 1 Action to command, which gives each creature in the Swarm 2 Actions to perform their turn. The difference being that the Swarm creatures can't be given complex commands, or perform specific things, they must all be commanded as a group. Group commands like "Attack that target", or "Defend this position", or other variants, ensure that the Swarm will always be performing the same simple activity when summoned.

Summons can be controlled from a great range, but it's not unlimited. For the most part that range includes the entire visible battlefield. A rough estimate for when summons begin to lose their vital link to their masters tends to be around 500ft, unless stated otherwise.

Players gain one skill point each level up, to spend where they see fit. Players starting at level 1 will have 3 skill points to spread out in any skill they wish.

Appearance

There's many different small fiefs that compose the greater united confederation that is the Ashen Lords. As such, many of them appear to hail from different walks of life. Some sport dark hair and olive skin, others pale complexion and lighter tones of brush, while some others appear much more exotic in their own ways. Another factor that serves as a distinction is between regular citizens, and the knights who have taken the Ashen Vow, those of which will often have a much more muscular, striated appearance. Suffice to say, many walks of life can be found amidst the ranks of the Ashen Lords, oftentimes it's not the color of your skin that matters, but the color of your banner.

History

The war torn lands of the Ashen Lords never truly knew prosperity before the rule of the first King Goldstein. As far back as they could remember, the clans, tribes, fiefs and landholders staked their claims and fought bloody battles over any scraps, some managing to hold on to more than others, until their titles started to sound official. Countless clans rose and fell during these battles, but the only names truly worth remembering are the ones who survived up until today, and one would do well to remember them, as the sycophants who cling to their banners enjoy some measure of survival in doing so.

One name shines brighter than all others however, a name that managed the impossible, to unite the clans and crown themselves as a royal lineage, Goldstein. Royalty has a different weight to one's name than a noble lineage, even if the lines blur as to how one acquires this title, the summit that crowned the first Goldstein King placed upon his shoulders a privilege and a burden that he could never truly know the true extent of. Still, the first years of rule were peaceful, even the long standing blood feud of Azuros and Carmine was able to be handled, if not mostly quelled. The new royalty acted as an executive power that checked the lords and managed their conflicts, ensuring that even some smaller houses could be allowed parity and just existence rather than simply being devoured.

The Goldsteins did not do this freely out of goodwill, however. Their lineage had always been fixated on a particular obsession. To find God, Heaven, Eden. The Goldstein family had always been peculiarly religious, always entertaining prophets from the Glassfolk alongside Edenists to preach and educate them on matters of theology. This of course would only lead to one place, Dust, and the origin of Dust, the Glass Desert. The first crusades saw the creation of the Templar order to journey to the Glass Desert in an expedition to find Eden, to find the Celestials, to find the answers that the Goldsteins so hungrily sought out. A few things can certainly be said about them: they made the Templar order more powerful, almost as powerful and rich as a great house. The Templars were unbound within Glassfolk territory, and the atrocities they committed in the name of finding Eden and harvesting Celestial Tears to the local population are without number.

King Arthur Goldstein had a fanatical fascination with the beyond, he entertained many hearings with high ranking members of the church to speak of matters beyond death. His vast library of knowledge grew, but it swelled in proportion to his anxiety. Dust is a dangerous thing, it can open pathways to many powers that are best left untouched, and the whispers that King Arthur heard, that drew him further and further in, were not those of God. What was brought out of his experiments could only be described as the work of The Devil.

Arthur had become a demon. He had unleashed horrors into the world beyond the comprehension of those who had so faithfully paved this path for him. The halls of Goldstein became twisted and marred with bloodsoaked massacre, and the throne was a breeding ground for further depravity. It would be the very order that the Goldstein throne had created and funded which would be the key to his downfall.

The Templars had not shared all of their spoils with the Goldstein crown. From Dust, they had created Ash, a substance that proved to be the antithesis of Dust, one that protected their knights from its influence, and gave them the strength to destroy the demons of Goldstein. They recruited the aid of the Azuros, who were horrified at the transformation of their previous liege. They also attempted to recruit the help of Carmine, but the Carmine lord did not match the aid. Instead he focused on defending his own territory, and observed.

Without the force they would have hoped for, the Templars and Azuros had a difficult fight ahead, but the power of Ash proved to be a deciding factor. To say victory came easy would be a lie however, and once the demon king lay in tatters, Duke Azuros would now be faced with an even harsher battle: to stay the High Marshal's hand as the Templars thirsted for the blood of every last Goldstein, regardless of their innocence.

But here is where lord Carmine would make his opportune appearance, fresh, his troops ready to exert control of a battlefield that had already been conquered and strained. Surrounding both Templar and Azuros troops, Carmine rallied to the aid of his long standing enemy and supported the position to show mercy to the Princess of Goldstein. Both the Duke and the High Marshal sensed that Carmine had further reasons to want this, but they had no recourse. Shortly after, the marriage announcement of Baron Carmine and Princess Goldstein would be declared. But that is a story for another time. The Goldstein throne had been re-established, the Carmine house had been elevated to a status of nobility which it had hardly scraped by before, and the Treatise of Ash was created. Due to the marriage alliance between Goldstein and Carmine, the Baron Carmine had a sway over the terms of the treatise that the Templars and Azuros did not appreciate, but the terms were signed all the same, creating a document that detailed tight control over Dust, mages, and the usage of Ash as their primary form of consolidated power. A clean power, one that burned away the influence of The Devil.

Many generations passed till we arrived at the current age, and the Great Houses of Ash maintain their hegemony even through the advancing technological progress that pushes into an era of modernized warfare. During the incursions of the Glass Desert, the Templars first came into contact with Berettan soldiers who carried automatic firearms, mechanized vehicles, radio vox communications, and even Dust powered technological circuits. This plundered technology was used and reverse engineered with time by branches of both the church and the great houses, each eager to get their hands on any possible advantage over the other, despite some of which skirting the lines of the treatise…

The balance of power within the social structure of the Ashen Lords is delicate. Very delicate.

Religion

The question of religion is complex for the Ashen Lords, for it was put to the test during the event of the Cataclysm, when a mad king was tempted by great power, and used Dust to bring about unparalleled destruction, never seen before.

Dust is miraculous, it is quite clearly something that allows mortal man to hold in their fingertips the power to transform reality. Something that truly, only God should possess. Those that taste the Dust consume a forbidden fruit of knowledge, and glimpse into the Eden that was not meant to be seen by mortal eyes, witnessing the possibilities that lay within it. This temptation however, is not natural. It's one that is guided into our minds by The Devil, and the constructions of Dust that mages bring forth with their vile machinations, are simply The Devil working through them.

One cannot simply discard Dust altogether however, as Dust is divine. Perhaps it is simply our minds that allow The Devil to work as an agent of chaos when we wander too close to the power of God. Mortals were never meant to eat the forbidden fruit after all, the secrets they glimpsed from within were simply too destructive.

That is why we take the Vow of Ash. To closely guard the people, and to closely watch the mages as they explore the secrets of the Garden. Should one slip too far, our blades will never be beyond reach.

The Vow of Ash: The candidate enters a sealed chamber with a vial of Ash salts, an Ash candle, and a brew of Ash tea. The ceremony lasts for the duration of the candle's wick. The candidate must breathe in the entirety of the fumes, drink the full brew, and wash themselves with the purifying salts in full. Mortals are naturally compatible with Dust, the Ash blocks all of those possible connections before they can be formed, or destroys them after they are already formed. Those that complete the Vow have found their own bodies to become miraculous in return. The strength, speed, endurance, and sheer power that the Ash provides them, makes them into an unstoppable fighting force.

The Four Great Houses

There are 4 Great Houses that govern the Ashen Lords. Each house has a somewhat different view on Dust, its uses, and how the mages should be treated. As such they come to an agreement and sign the Treatise of Ash, to determine the laws that govern the feuds between the houses, be them great or minor. At present, the balance of power between them is frail, held together perhaps only by the ironclad promise of the Treatise of Ash, and the knowledge that there are more despicable enemies out there, than each other.

House Goldstein · The Crown
A long time ago, the Goldstein family united all of the greater and minor houses within a single banner. That was until a ruinous king abused the power of Dust and became a demon. They have since recovered their political power, and rule their house with vast wealth. Perhaps it is folly, but they insist on the research of Dust, even considering the mistakes their ancestors followed. The line between skirting the demands of the Treatise and blatantly misinterpreting it, is often too thin for the liking of their Templar neighbors.
The Templars · The Church Militant
The Templar order was created by a saint who developed the Vow of Ash to put down the demon that came forth due to the sin of Goldstein's hubris. They are a puritan order which believes that Dust is nothing but a temptation of The Devil. Templar owned lands are extremely dangerous to traveling mages, those deemed as witches are quick to be gathered up and summarily executed, often by burning at the stake, or crucifixion. Templars have also been known to employ the use of Stigmatas and Penitents, abhorrent abominations created using experiments of Ash, devoid of will, only obedience.
House Azuros · The Lions
Honor, duty and glory. The Azuros are one of the oldest houses, their lineage traced back to some of the first landclaims before the Goldstein banner. During the Goldstein reign many Azuros knights were chosen as royal protectors, proven time and time again to be men and women of unwavering commitment and loyalty. But all of that trust would be broken when their king betrayed them. The Azuros have an extremely strong sense of honor, and a martial tradition that goes back thousands of years, one that never relied on Dust even before the creation of the Vow of Ash. To them it is a matter of pride, a lion's pride, that battles be settled in the old ways, the way of the knight.
House Carmine · The Opportunists
The Carmines rose to power from nothing, struggling to grasp their station during an age where blood meant everything. House Carmine does not hold the most favorable reputation amongst the other Ashen Lords, but that hardly matters when their forces and resources allow them to shout out their demands boldly and proudly. Their first leader was of lowborn birth, recruiting prisoners, convicts, thieves and bandits. All the lowlives that had no place to go found a home under Carmine, and their methods would not be shunned as long as they got results. Today, the Carmines often keep mages as slaves, using them as battlefield tools, or sometimes even pets.

Appearance

There's two types of Wildlanders that one might encounter when venturing far north. First would be the Aesir and the other are known as the Vanir. The Aesir are giants, they tower over all other mortals, possessing power and strength equal to no other. Aesir might stand between 9 to 12 feet tall, with long and packed limbs full of power. They are more than capable of wrestling wild beasts with their bare hands, and often do, making a show of it to prove their superior power. As a northern race, the Aesir usually sport lighter colors for their hair, eyes, and skin tones.

Meanwhile the Vanir possess animal characteristics, they are mystifying, and exude magic in their exuberant forms. The Vanir are often much more magically talented than the Aesir, their druidic aptitude is often very apparent even early on in their lives, with even small Vanir kids already playing magical tricks on their disapproving and frustrated parents. Vanir look human for the most part, but a plethora of secondary animal-like characteristics can manifest, seemingly at random, but usually following the ones the parents have. Tails, ears, teeth, eyes, skin… Amongst others are known to decorate the Vanir and make them appear uniquely colorful. Both kinds of Wildlanders have incredible innate magic inside of them, and this is shown in their rather bewitching forms.

History

The history of the Wildlanders is sparse. Their recorded tales are passed down orally for the most part from tribe to tribe, which means that the true thread of events is shaky at best at times, and much of the happenings ends up melting away into epics of colossal proportions, oft exaggerated by the giant skalds who sing of them.

Above all are the Primordials, they are the will of nature itself, the living flesh of the Gods, as wherever they go they bring about calamity and disaster. The very air blows differently at the beat of a Primordial's wings, winter or summer have no meaning when a Primordial can change it with a single breath, shifting an entire ecosystem with their passing. But despite all that, a Primordial's passing is a signal of rebirth. After the storm dies down and it returns to its nest, the land re-awakens so filled with life, infused with Dust in such an extravagant manner, that the reactionary bloom is a jarring jolt in the other direction.

Not all tribes are so fortunate to see this rebirth however. As was the case of the Aesir tribe long ago, where the leader did not see the signs of a coming Primordial soon enough, and his entire village was ravaged by the beast. The only survivors of the attack were his two sons, Thor and Loki.

These youth were left abandoned to the wilds to fend for themselves, but in the wake of a Primordial's ruin, nature always blossoms. They built themselves back up in the way their ancestors meant. They hunted for food, and with the bones of the prey they killed, they were able to hunt larger and stronger game. The brothers could never quite forget the pain they felt for losing their clan however, and as they grew stronger from their hunts, they felt the need to track down the fierce Primordial who wiped their home off the map.

As they traveled, the brothers would find others who would join them. The drive to hunt the Primordial who took everything from them spurring them forward, but the growing numbers ever weighing them down to settle. As more seasons passed by, the camp of the brothers had grown too big to move. They had settled in a mountain pass, and began to use it as a central hub of exploration for expeditions further into the deep north, all the while their main camp blossomed into a city.

But the true test of the fortress would come when the Primordial came back. It was a gargantuan thing, with black wings that blotted the sun, and a wingbeat that could be felt for days. They saw the signs, animals changing course, migrating, shifting, even the very trees were withering as the Primordial approached. But they had settled here, they would not move now. They would stay their ground and slay the beast no matter the cost. At the end, they were victorious, wounding it enough to send it reeling back to the far reaches of the icy tundra.

The brothers were exhausted, but both of them felt as if this was the only time to chase the monster into its lair. They led an expedition of their most trusty hunters to the furthest reaches of the north, tracking the blood of the beast. As they finally reached the cavern where they had tracked the wounded Primordial, not many of the original party had been left alive, but still they pressed on. Here, the story is split however. Only Thor and Loki descended into the deepest cave. Only Thor came back. He climbed back up clutching his hammer, and Loki's broken bow. When Loki's companions demanded to know what had happened to their friend, Thor flew into a rage, he killed one of them.

"Only Thor came back… He climbed back up clutching his hammer, and Loki's broken bow."

Upon returning to the stronghold Thor carved a crown out of bones that no one had ever seen before, and declared himself the King of the North, saying that the Wildlands would be under his protection forever, and that no tribe, as long as they bowed down to him, would ever fear a Primordial ever again. The followers of Loki grew disgruntled with his claims of course, choosing to leave and find their own answers. And still to this day they hunt and raid and take from the Throne, in an endless conflict.

Religion & Society

The religious beliefs of the Wildlanders profoundly connect them to nature, and the forms in which nature manifests, both beautiful and frightening. They are polytheistic, with a pantheon so vast and wondrous, explaining each act of nature as the kindness, fury, or trick of one of their majestic gods. Their very existence as a highly magically innate race fuels their belief that theirs is a land of mythos and gods, a land blessed like no other.

Wildlander society is largely meritocratic, tribal, and nomadic. The ones who provide most value for the tribe are fit to lead and make decisions. Some more successful tribes have managed to settle, and create more permanent lodgings that serve as trading hubs between traveling bands and even other nations. The largest of the trading hubs occupies a crucial pass, and for many generations the tribe that built and prospered over it has seen fit to crown their ruler as the King or Queen of the North. It's a title that, to some, is seen as a formality of a tribal leader that has over reached their station, and to others, an affront, or perhaps a spark of jealousy.

The Major Tribes

The Throne of the Grand Pass
What started out perhaps thousands of years ago as a small settlement at a key pass slowly grew into a bustling hall, a fortress, a pinnacle of solidified power. The clans that took hold here were for the most part of Aesir rule, as these tend to be most strongly keen on shows of force and dominance, struggling to prove their superiority with trophies and displays of conquest. The Druids seek to preserve, protect, and study the ways of nature.

The Hunt is everything to some Wildlanders, even to the point of breaking taboo. The Wild Hunt is a movement of raiders that ignores these conventions, gathering followers who seek to take from the weak and prove their strength of arms, to raid and hunt until they are satisfied, even if that hunt takes them to a Primordial. The Wild Hunt raiders are usually not welcome in most civilized settlements, especially not within the Throne, and are actively at odds with the Crown of the North.

Republic of the Free Cities

The RFC · Industrial Democracy
DemocraticMagitechIndustrialSecular

Appearance

People from all walks of life are found in the Republic, as this is a place that welcomes anyone that's hungry to discover the inner workings of the world. Hungry for progress, discovery and technological advancements. Here you might find all sorts of different people walking side by side. The most striking feature that sets the people of the Republic apart tends to be their dress, which is, at least in the upper parts of the city, a rather striking and revolutionary form of fashion that isn't seen in many other places of the world. In the lower city, this fashion is echoed in a more ragged manner, creating its own aesthetic.

History

The history of the early Republic has been somewhat lost to time, as the records of each individual city state, minor trade federation, kingdom, and even sparse villages that were brought into the fold of a single centralized power structure. The records that we do have talk about a somewhat convoluted, long winded process, one that did not involve any armed conflicts, but more so trade agreements and modernization practices that had further and further outreach, until the full archipelago of the Republic was under one ruling government.

Most of the documents that speak of the early republic's access to Dust came from trading with the Melistead for Abyssal Ichor. The research of this substance and the qualities that it had provided a much greater demand for the substance, one that would fuel the fires of industry and discovery. But this would also mean that the Ichor delivered by the Melisteads was no longer enough, and as such the republic would need to find new sources of Dust.

The industrial revolution that pushed the republic forward also enabled it to travel and chart the world at a speed which was not possible for other nations. The use of magitech engines to propel vehicles through the land, water and even the air would greatly enable the republic's rapid globalization. This however was not free of cost. The enthusiasm that propelled these technological marvels was carried upon the backs of the populace, which were exploited by a myriad of technocratic bureaucrats, government officials, scientists, and wealthy investors who cared only for the speed in which new products could be made with the miraculous Dust, not to the many hazards that the workers were suffering due to the increasingly horrid conditions they were forced to endure.

The production bottlenecks would become increasingly common, as crime intensified, syndicates formed to organize strikes and workers rights movements, and eventually even a political party formed with enough backing to bring these problems to the senate. A big win for the populace, but an undeniable headache for the technocrats up top. But that's also when their navigations and chartings began to bear real fruit. They would find the massive landmass of the Glass Desert, establish connections with the oasis Glassfolk, and saw that this place was extremely rich in Dust.

The reality of the matter was much different though. The desert itself was a hellish deathtrap. Soldiers reported the heat within the traversal vehicles to be unbearable, and some would often even collapse, cooked inside of what was now effectively an oversized, expensive oven. They would also be met with resistance, not just from the locals, but also other foreign nations who sought to harvest the precious Dust. Many skirmishes over harvesting sites would happen on the daily, and despite the republic's technological superiority, they would time and time again find out they were horrifyingly outmatched. To the inexperienced peacekeeper of the republic who had only ever seen combat against a rioter or in a safe drill exercise, the reality of war within the glass sands was brutal.

The veterans of these conflicts would inevitably learn, grow, adapt. Their technology would improve. But that was one bottleneck taken care of, the others would remain, and only grow with time. The war veterans who returned home after the desert campaigns were paranoid with what they saw, and created a political party of their own, seeking to arm the citizens and prepare the republic as a more capable nation. Their fearmongering worked, and collected much support, solidifying a foundational tri-partisan system that balances a very delicate tightrope of influence.

Religion & Society

The Republic allows free worship of any religion within their walls, but the government has a very hardline separation of church and state. The governing body is a secular democracy which values the scientific proof of natural phenomena, as such viewing religion and spirituality as something unnecessary for progress.

The Republic is governed by several political parties that lobby the interests of various groups. Society at large is divided by economic classes that separate the citizenry in rather literal levels of living, where the upper cities house the wealthy elite, the important inventors, the merchant tycoons and the ruling politicians. Even further below are the workers, who tend to get the short end of the stick, living in absolute poverty, areas of low policing which also devolve into violent crime and gang territories.

The Three Parties

People's Workers Party (WP)
A party that has garnered a lot of political impetus especially in the latest years due to a greater consciousness to the concerns over workers rights, as well as a demand for religious freedoms in the lower cities. The WP has a particularly strong religious backing from the Church. Their political missions are generally to improve the worker's living conditions in the factories, to raise government social aid initiatives, and to implement religious education within state mandated programs.
Future Foundation Party (FFP)
A party that has always been one of the backbones of the Republic's very identity. The FFP owns many of the high tech corporations that research advancements in all fields of magic and science, and most lobby the government funding to further these advancements for the betterment of society. Their biggest current project is an arcane gateway, a teleportation device. Another recently completed project by a scientist named Robert Turing was the Homunculus Project, an idea that aimed to create artificial, lab fabricated, synthetic humans. These homunculi were a subject of much debate when they were revealed to the public, and still are under heavy scrutiny.
National Responsibility Party (NRP)
A party that tends to gain frontrunner support during times of global strife. The NRP tends to advocate for tighter restrictions on immigration policies, border control, and military force. The Republic's army isn't the most impressive, they need to make up for it in technological might. The NRP always advocates for a generous budget to be spent on military research and development.

The Kingdom of Heaven

Appearance

There's two major ethnicities in the Kingdom of Heaven, those of Fire Blood, and those of Iron Blood. To outsiders these two appear somewhat similar, but the culture inside the Kingdom will quickly disperse these notions. Firebloods tend to look slightly more delicate, rounder features, softer, skin resembling porcelain and most often sport amber eyes. The Ironbloods are decidedly more rough, with tan skin, dark eyes, and sharper features. Both ethnicities have very dark hair, with very few rare exceptions.

History

In the beginning there were two nations, one of Iron and one of Fire. The nation of Iron was forged by battle, they held their own against neighboring provinces who took their livestock and raided their fields, evolving and perfecting the art of war against each other. To their south was the nation of Fire, who had a deeply religious devotion to a magnificent tree that did not grow leaves, instead it blazed with a golden flame, bathing them in an eternal warm glow.

The nation of Fire had the gift of magic, a gift that was bestowed upon them by the tree. The tree would often blaze with such intensity that it would shed crystals, ones that the people would treasure, and later would learn to consume, as it was the fruit of the tree after all, its gift of divinity. They would shape their land, create majestic monuments, transform themselves, ascend. They would become more than mortal, all because of the fruits of the tree, and because the tree chose them, they would see it as their divine right to rule. The nation of Iron, primitive as they were without access to the miraculous gifts of the tree, could not stand up to magic of that scale. They were caught by surprise, and were eventually worn down and subjugated.

The restructuring of the two nations into one would grind the dreams of expansion to a sudden halt. The theocracy that once ruled as the circle of the Divine Tree, transformed into a Ministry, as high ranking nobles turned their vast, magically enhanced lifespans, ironically, to a practical, non magical study of law. The answer to many of these problems would come from within the tree itself, as it spoke. Rather unexpectedly, someone stepped out from within, a being draped in fire who addressed the ones who had been worshiping the tree for so long. The being told them about Dust, about how the fruits of the tree were Dust, and about how other places in this world had Dust. The being disappeared inside of the tree once again, but the Ministry flourished the base of the tree with a palace, creating a massive, opulent location for the being to come to should it wish to once again grace them with their presence.

Things would grind to a halt when the demon appeared from the north, the twisted mockery of the king of their enemies, who corrupted all lands that he touched. The tree's flames began to dwindle for the first time, and the Divine Heir stepped out of the tree, demanding more Dust. Members of the Ministry experimented with Dark Magic as they witnessed what was possible, and greed spoke louder than any reason. The flames of the tree dwindled further still, the treasonous traitors were executed, their heads placed on pikes displayed at the city gates.

But undeniably, greed spoke louder. A drop of blood was all it took, and the Rite of Embers was created. Blood like Fire, well and truly, ascension to divinity, something so beautiful it felt sacrilegious to even touch it. But none of the Masters would dare to resist it, every Fireblood noble coveted the secrets, it became status, symbol, power.

Conversely, the secrets of Ash would be stolen as time went on, an outrage, barbarity, a mockery of all they stood for. And one they would start to liberally use as a punishment to brand prisoners and outcasts. A foolish act that would backfire time and time again, as the Ministry, in their hubris, tended to forget that their enemies developed Ash to be a weapon after all.

Power Factions

Twelve generals of legendary prowess who pass down this title and legend to worthy prospects. The demands of the military had to be heard, sometimes, irritatingly so.

Merchant lords, diplomats, and trade barons who have painstakingly worked to forge a meticulous network so vast and extensive that without the Diamonds holding it all together, the Kingdom could face a rather severe crisis. The Forsaken
There are many within the Kingdom who are discarded and forgotten. The lawless, the Ashbloods, any and all who do not fit the societal mold that the caste system created. Even the regiment of Ashbloods, which despite earning the scorn of the rest of the army, still stands strong, and keeps itself alive despite all odds.

The Rite of Embers

A core pillar of the society built upon the back of magical power, was always to cultivate an even higher understanding of it, to live and breathe Dust, to become Dust. The Rite of Embers is the truest form of magical awakening that Dust can bring to an individual, it opens every single circuit within a person's body to an infinite amount of potential, allowing power to flow freely from chakra to chakra, bestowing them with endless possibilities.

The Rite has a few variations which tend to be guarded within the sects of the most powerful Fireblood families, but secrets have a way of slipping out, even to the point that some in the Republic have found ways to synthesize the process. It is also different depending on the age of the undertaking. Regardless of the method used, the Rite consists of a great deal of Ember Dust being consumed, mixing with the candidate's blood, and being allowed to flow freely until their blood is fully that of fire.

Appearance

The Children of Glass, or banū az-zujāj as they call themselves in their native tongue, are largely of two separate ethnicities, human and orc, having mixed for many generations, and some more prevalent in certain parts of the desert than others.

The Orc Glassfolk are taller, more muscular, their skin has a rougher texture that seems to be built to survive the desert better than most humans, standing at around 5'10 to 6'4 feet tall. Orc Glassfolk who live deeper in the Glass Desert are usually darker, with lighter green tones mostly seen with those who live in the oasis, shores, or even outside the desert itself.

Human Glassfolk tend to be darker of skin, eyes and hair, due to the extreme heat and intense climate of their lands. The unforgiving nature of the desert leaves them with rough features, especially when venturing to the tribes deeper inside the Glass Desert. In general however, they're slightly shorter than other human races, standing at 5'4 to 6 feet tall, and are incredibly slim. It's very rare to see someone from the desert with much body mass whatsoever.

History

Life has always been harsh in the desert. Every day is a struggle for survival, for water, for food, for preservation of the way of life. The way of life itself is a blessing, and while the sun that rises high in the sky above brings great pain, it's also a blessing from God, proof that this world has been blessed with life. The trials that have been laid upon the people of glass have been many, but God is good, for they have made his people strong.

The history of the Glassfolk is sparse, even more so because the communities that dwell deep within the sands all have their own stories. It's hard to keep communication between each village, and they all have their own leaders and customs, which lead to a very decentralized society. It might come together in times of great strife, but to push them to do so would be a great disaster indeed.

But things would change for the Glassfolk when the greed of outsiders was set upon their sands, as many foreign powers began to set harvesting operations, expeditions, and to walk where they were not welcome. These foreign invaders would set foot upon sands which are not theirs, and spill indiscriminate blood to bring their treasures back to their homeland. They carve out the desert for Dust, with no regard for any who dwell here.

"These sands have seen many come and go, they will see many come and go still… But someday a prophet will unite the ones who dwell upon the sands, and drive away the invaders who only seek to make this land suffer."

Religion

The Children of Glass believe in only one true God, the creator deity, who forges good people with many hardships, and rewards them with Dust, so they may see miracles and prosper. They see the hand of God in many things, and even the Celestials that roam the desert, are they not a manifestation of His divinity? A work of His miracles? They give us the Dust for which we may also taste Heaven.

Society & Factions

The society of the Children of Glass is theocratic in nature, with the priests and wise elders occupying the higher echelons of society. Because of the isolated nature of their communities, there's no centralized governing power that rules over them as a whole. Instead, each community defers to the ruling of 3, a Warrior, a Priest, and an Artisan. The more secular communities that live in the shores and oasis however, tend to be ruled by a merchant class which has risen above the traditionalist structure, and uses their wealth and influence to swing decisions with the foreign nations who wish to partake in the trade of Dust.

It is said that a long time ago the Glassfolk were a single tribe, who ventured deep into the Glass Desert in a holy mission to follow the Celestial's trail of tears in order to find the hidden Oasis of Eden. Despite wandering for years, that mission proved to be so harsh and so unforgiving, that many tribes broke off from the wandering pilgrimage. Many returned to the safety of the shores and oasis, settling the edges of the Glass Desert, while others would continue, deeper and deeper, finding ways to survive even in that horrific landscape.

The Procession of Sacred Tears
These are the religious fundamentalists that have settled deep in the Glass Desert, who worship the Celestials, and who have spent generations upon generations on the search for the Oasis of Eden. Their homes lay deep within canyons and crevices, built in such a way that they can use the rocky formations of the earth to ventilate the brutal heat, conserving water, and a simple way of life. All of this hardens them. No one knows better how to survive deep in the glass desert than those of the tribes of the Procession.
The Bountiful Oasis Coalition
In contrast, the Glassfolk who chose to settle outside of the desert, on the fringes of the brutal sands, tend to live a much richer and fulfilled lifestyle, "cushy" as some others would put it. The access to plentiful water as well as the ports allow them to trade for the things they lack, which means they often want for nothing, especially not customers to buy their Dust. The biggest difference here is that they see the Celestials' motions as far less sacred, and are far more willing to sell the precious tears to outsiders who pay vast riches for them.

Desert Gear

Living in the deepest parts of the Glass Desert is not as simple as the rest of the world, especially when it comes to resources. Weapons, tools, armor, even food and drink need to be especially made, preserved or treated to properly serve the climate.

After Dust, water is the most precious resource in the desert. Without water one will die within a day, dehydrated and left out to be plucked clean by the scavengers. The Glassfolk have developed many tools over the years to extract and preserve water, either from the scarce desert plantlife, or other living things. One of the primary methods by which they collect water is from dew, which they use a multilayered, Dust woven fabric that traps the cool night air inside, condensing it and sucking up the moisture through a tube. Water can also be collected from plants, corpses, or even moist soil in desperate circumstances, via a syringe-like extractor which filters impurities and siphons moisture. Glassfolk primarily eat dried palm dates, goat dairy, desert roots, and desert prey such as hare, birds, and insects. They eat a single light meal during the day so as to not overwork the stomach, and a heavier meal at night to recuperate the energy.

The armor of the Glassfolk is never too heavy, always light, prioritizing speed to move with the shifting sands. Metal armor heats up too quickly in the desert, so it's avoided, even those that wear heavier sets in the middle of the desert tend to favor chitin material scavenged from the large insectoids that roam the wastes. Weapons follow similar principles, as smelting is difficult in the desert, chitin, bone, wood, and obsidian is much more commonly used.

Appearance

The Melistead Elves, or simply known as Melisteads, have the signature physical trait of having pointed ears. They are holders of ethereal beauty, eyes with shades of blues and greens, much like the ocean being trapped. They are a tall race, with their height ranging from the shortest being 1.75 meters to the tallest being 2.18 meters, regardless of gender. Their skin is smooth and pale against the deep blue ocean, with their hair differing shades from the darkest of blues to the deep green colors. It is said that a pureblooded Melistead will have these traits, and this is often found among the nobility, most heavily in the royal family of Melistead.

Since Melisteads are a race of long-lived creatures, those from other heritage have slowly trickled into a select few lineages, where a Melisteadian feature can vary. Some will have the Melistead eyes only, or some will have the signature hair. Some will have none of the blessing of having the shades of the ocean appear, but all will always have the pointed ears.

Another physical trait of the Melisteads is when they are underwater. Those of Melistead descent are blessed by their gods where they can breathe underwater. Their pointed ears become like the fins of fishes, and a part of their pale skin will turn a tint of ocean blue and their hair, regardless of the color on land, turns blue. A Melistead can see underwater with their eyes normally, but the deeper down the ocean they go, they use sound as a way to see (sonar), and a well trained Melistead can incorporate this skill above land as well. Physically, Melistead Elves vary in body types depending on their occupation, but the consensus is that a Melistead will always thrive in water.

"Water is fluid, soft and yielding. But water will wear away rock, which is rigid and cannot yield."

History

According to the records found in the sacred Balete Tree, the first Melisteads rose from the depths of the ocean, riding on the back of different sea monsters. They were given life from the blood that dropped in the ocean after Bathala Maykapal was wounded by the Bakunawa, a giant Sea Serpent and a former god, who ate his daughter, the Bathaluman Mayari, goddess of the Moon, resulting in the moon's white glow turning into a red ominous color. After the war, the deities transformed the Bakunawa's remains into the islands, which reflected a crescent moon's shape. This became the home of the elves where they developed their society.

During Lakan Afamrail I's reign, the elves discovered the existence of a magic source so pure and dangerous the elves thought that this was from a creature born of the Bakunawa's blood. Divers found a sea bed filled with a luminous blue liquid at about 4,000 meters deep. But it was also in this discovery that they realized the horror that was underneath the sea they called home. The creatures there were enormous, three times the size of an elf's whole body. Their Arael'Sha were the ones who pulled them away and swam up to the surface as fast as they could, not all given that mercy. These creatures would eventually be called the Abyssals, and the luminous blue liquid they exude from their bodies, the Abyssal Ichor.

The farthest a research and diver team has gone down is about 6,500 meters, that research was immediately halted as the coral nest found by the team had a rare Abyssal creature they named the Lyvathan, a serpent creature who's as big as the Bakunawa. It was only the second time it had been spotted after centuries of research.

Contact with the outside world came when Timao Lamruil, a Melistead Prince, was training by the edge of the ocean territory near the wall of fog when he saw a man floating unconsciously. The man was brought to the castle and saved. Due to the elves being cut off from all other nations, their language was entirely different from what the man spoke. Not losing hope, Timao Lamruil spent time with this man day and night, creating a deep bond and finding ways to communicate. Word by word, phrase by phrase, he was finally able to have a coherent conversation with the man — named Taisong — who was from the Kingdom of Heaven, part of an expedition to navigate the world of Babel. After almost a decade of recovery and preparation, Taisong was escorted home. Before leaving, Timao Lamruil gave him a personal gift, a gemstone called Aquamarine, infused with Abyssal Ichor, attached to a platinum cuff bracelet, with a promise that they would see each other once again.

Three years later, Timao Lamruil found himself facing a large warship emerging from the wall of fog — the single warship that survived the high tidal waves and storm and successfully navigated through the dense fog. Reinforcements were called, and a large naval army gathered, led by Timao Lamruil's eldest sister, the queen Hara Aloiene IV, on the back of a sea serpent whose last recorded measurement was 179 metres. What is Dust?

Dust is the most valuable substance in the world, it's the source of magic, and since magic can change the world in unnatural ways, it is also the source of miracles. The Dust is carried in the bones of the ancients that walked this world long before we did, the Celestials, Abyssals, and Primordials. Dust is the substance that allows us mere mortals to channel their magic, to make use of their miracles, and to shape the world with divine power.

Because of its miraculous nature, Dust has been the most valuable substance known to man for as long as we have walked this earth. Wars have been fought over the control of Dust, debates have been had over the morality of its use, and even today the world hangs at a precarious balance as many try to forcefully control the flow of Dust into their own coffers.

When ingested over the course of training and meditation, Dust will cause a person to slowly but surely form magical circuits within their body, allowing them to make use of magic. The more Dust someone ingests over their life, and especially the more high quality Dust, they develop more acute and powerful circuits, being able to use more advanced magic as a result.

Harvesting

Dust Harvesting is inherently tied to the creatures that produce Dust, which are the Celestials, Abyssals, and Primordials. Without which, there would be no Dust in this world.

Celestial Tears can only be found in the Glass Desert and come from the wandering Celestials who roam the very deepest parts of the desert. However there is one pattern that every Celestial will do, which can only be described as prayer. When the sun reaches its zenith, at a heat so brutal that it can even cause spontaneous combustion in the areas directly affected by the light, a wandering Celestial will look directly up in worship. In this motion, they will begin to weep clear golden crystals that scatter on the sands below, these crystals are the Celestial Tears, some of the most valuable and highly concentrated sources of Dust in the world. Experienced Dust harvesters know that the timing is crucial at this moment, the Celestials do not like to be interrupted on their prayer. If they move to collect too early, they risk incurring the wrath of the Celestial, which is tantamount to suicide. Old Celestials are able to turn people into glass with their gaze alone.

Another greatly valuable source of Dust is from Abyssal Ichor, found in the deep sea, on the Abyssal coral reefs that serve as their nests. The substance in question is a bright blue ooze that contains a high concentration of Dust particles. It's created as the Abyssals drag their prey down to drown, binding them to large fungal colonies within the depths of the coral reefs, creating a strange and mesmerizing kaleidoscope of blue hues. It can be an eerie, terrifying task to dive that deep into the abyss, where the only light that you see might just be your prize, or the lure of a creature that will drag you even further down.

Primordial Dust tends to be a bit more roundabout to come by, as they don't provide a direct means of harvesting like the others. What does happen is that a Primordial tends to bless the lands they claim as their territory with a great bounty of natural Dust. The very presence of a Primordial within a land can cause the flora to become infused with Dust, which then makes the animals carry Dust in their blood, flesh, and bones. The gardens of the Primordials are rich and full of primal life, edens of growth and primeval magic.

Dust Technology

The industrial revolution of Dust in the technological sector started with the discovery of the Dust based circuit, a research in Republica which sought to apply magic Dust into a more practical form of usage, one that didn't depend on a trained mage to be able to perform it. These researchers concluded that some circuits can be replicated and interfaced in a manner at which they can be interacted with without the usual ingestion and subsequent development of internal circuits, like a mage would.

This discovery was rather revolutionary because it expanded the understanding of what an enchantment was, building upon the principle of it at a baseline, but manipulating it to function in a far more practical manner. As the circuitry advanced, many new avenues of discovery were made available, vox recording and radio broadcasting for example, analog screen interfaces for machine monitors, moving engines, mechanical parts, robotics, cybernetics, and even more.

Lust — The Man Who Loved A Painting

Caelus was a handsome merchant who collected works of art. His mansion was filled with paintings, sculptures, pottery, and all that could be called art. His earnings are always spent on auction houses bidding away on only the finest work of art. One day, after he made a bountiful earning from a noble who bought his merchandise, he went to the auction house and wondered what art he would find. There he saw a piece of painting of a copper haired woman with emerald eyes in a silken gown, kneeling on a marble floor, hands raised to the sky in prayer. He was mesmerized. So mesmerized that he bid all his earnings just to have this painting.

At home, he removed all the works of art he collected, telling his employees to sell them and use the money to find the painter. Ever since he brought home the painting Caelus was stricken with emotions and sensations he was unfamiliar with. He ignored his work, his family, and everything else and would sit in the room where the painting hung and would stare at it for hours, ignoring even the food served to him. Sometimes his servants would find him in the dead of night, touching the painting, kissing it, rubbing himself against it, and would whisper his desires to it.

When none of his employees were able to find the painter, he ordered them to find every single woman who resembles the painting, using every means necessary. Caelus used his riches to get these women and when brought to the room with the painting they would never come out. It wasn't known the exact amount, but the women never returned to their homes.

Several towns and cities became alarmed when women with similar features started disappearing from all over, authorities began their investigation, what they found was the most disturbing. In Caelus's mansion, in the room where the painting hung was Caelus and all the women brought to him. As if in a trance, they continued to drown themselves in the ecstasy of the flesh. Caelus now with eyes burned bright red, mouth filled with blood as he partook in his desires and their blood.

The church was called to exterminate Caelus, and the mansion became a breeding ground of demonic energy. But when it was taken for investigation, both men and women would write down something different. One woman said it was a man on his knees, flogging himself in sin, some men would say it was a woman with black hair, or blonde hair, kneeling and praying. The church, afraid that this painting would only create further chaos, sealed it and the whole mansion, where no civilian, without the church's permission, could ever set foot in or see the accursed painting.
